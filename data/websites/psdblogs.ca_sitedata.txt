&nbsp;Twenty one Superintendents were invited to join the <a href=\"http://www.c21canada.org/ceo-academy/\" target=\"_blank\">CEO Academy</a>&nbsp;to look at&nbsp;the future of education in Canada. &nbsp;<a href=\"https://www.psdblogs.ca/files/2015/05/Screen-Shot-2015-05-25-at-8.57.07-PM-19zwq7w.png\"><img class=\"alignright size-medium wp-image-754\" Learning Landscape in Canada</a>“, was released, authored by <a href=\"http://www.cea-ace.ca/users/penny-milton\" target=\"_blank\">Penny Milton</a> (former head of the <a href=\"http://www.cea-ace.ca/\" target=\"_blank\">Canadian Education Association of Canada</a>). &nbsp;All members of the CEO Academy contributed to the position paper that “…offers a vehicle for learning systems to reflect and inspire innovation that supports competency and resiliency in a global economy”.Recently one of my colleagues from the CEO Academy, <a href=\"http://www.timmonds.org/twitter.com/chrkennedy\" target=\"_blank\">Chris Kennedy</a>, blogged “<a href=\"http://cultureofyes.ca/2015/05/05/keeping-a-national-view/\" target=\"_blank\">Keeping a National View</a>“. &nbsp;Whether it be Chris or other members of the CEO Academy, it became clear &nbsp;to me, we have similar cultures being developed that allow each division to move forward in shifting our learning in Canada. &nbsp;In <a href=\"http://www.timmonds.org/psd70.ab.ca\" target=\"_blank\">Parkland School Division</a>, we have created an innovative culture where we see the necessity of learning together. &nbsp; Creating a team that is diversified and also focused on putting our children first in an ever changing world, have been keys to our growth. &nbsp;<a href=\"http://www.psd70.ab.ca/ourdivision/missionandvision/Pages/Default.aspx\" target=\"_blank\">Our vision of exploration, creativity, imagination, exciting learning and aspiring to reach ones dreams has brought us together as a division</a>. &nbsp;Speaking to one vision has been integral in growing our culture. &nbsp;Our vision/purpose united a team of dedicated educators creating a culture which accelerated amazing learning opportunities for all learners of PSD70.In <em>Parkland School Division,</em> we are focused on two priorities – <strong>engagement</strong> (students, staff, parents and community) and <strong>stewardship of resources</strong>. Our goals of inclusion, quality learning, culture of wellness, and universal leadership, continue to be embedded in all we do. &nbsp;</article>\n<article><a href=\"https://www.psdblogs.ca/files/2015/05/Goals2015-16455sp.jpeg\"><img class=\"aligncenter size-large wp-image-757\" src=\"https://www.psdblogs.ca/files/2015/05/Goals2015-16455sp-1024x1024.jpeg\" alt=\"Goals2015\" width=\"960\" height=\"960\" srcset=\"https://www.psdblogs.ca/files/2015/05/Goals2015-16455sp-1024x1024.jpeg 1024w, https://www.psdblogs.ca/files/2015/05/Goals2015-16455sp-150x150.jpeg 150w, https://www.psdblogs.ca/files/2015/05/Goals2015-16455sp-300x300.jpeg 300w\" sizes=\"(max-width: 960px) 100vw, 960px\"></a></article>\n<article>To be successful, our work must be embedded in our culture. &nbsp;It has become part of our daily work as we prepare, engage, and inspire our students to be their best in a quickly changing global community.Our school division <em>values and beliefs</em>, speak about learning for all; collaboration; engagement; open honest communication; trust; respect; leadership; excellence; innovation; risk taking; citizenship; safe and caring; &nbsp;and resiliency. &nbsp; All wonderful words yet&nbsp;they mean nothing without implementation. &nbsp;Here in PSD, no matter the position, we strive to bring these values/beliefs, as well as the mission, to life. Trustees and our senior executive team&nbsp;must model the culture we are striving for, but also we are open to learning from all stakeholders in our community. &nbsp; From there the culture grows.<p></p>\n<p>Parkland School Division students/staff and community are helping to grow a culture that is guided by what is in the best interest of our students. &nbsp;Here in PSD a culture of very little girl. I spent my time dreaming of being a teacher and those dreams got bigger when I listened to my grandma tell her stories of being a teacher is a one room school house, lighting the fire when she got there and using a hand cart on the railway to get to and from school. &nbsp;When I wasn’t dreaming, I was practicing by sitting my little sister in front of the chalkboard while I “played” school for hours and hours – always making sure that she was grasping the lessons that I was teaching (whatever they were).</p>\n<p>Then I was teaching, in front of a room full of children and I felt excited – at first. &nbsp;It wasn’t long before I realized that all of my students weren’t learning in the same way. &nbsp;I knew I wasn’t reaching some of them and I felt like I couldn’t manage some of the behaviors that were constantly present. &nbsp;I grew more and more anxious and it wasn’t long before I was feeling that if these students weren’t learning the way that I was teaching then they needed to be somewhere else with someone else – after all I was trained to do was I was doing so if I was in the right place these students were obviously not. &nbsp;I really cared about them and wanted them to love learning and love being at school so I advocated having them working in a place that could meet their needs because I didn’t know how. &nbsp;Looking back I now know that I had a very single sighted view of the world of teaching.</p>\n<p>So how did that change for me?</p>\n<p>Something really important happened in my life that was truly a gift. &nbsp;My third child developed a serious retinal disease when he was 1 and we had to travel to Boston over 30 times for eye surgeries and eventually he had to have his eyes removed. &nbsp;He has been blind since the age of 3. &nbsp;He started his school journey early as a Program Unit Funded child and as he progressed through the grades I learned more and more and began to see the school experience in a completely different way.</p>\n<p>I learned that the system isn’t designed broadly enough to authentically embrace all students. &nbsp;The focus is driven by mastery of content and the expected results are celebrated through academic achievement. The experience of parenting my son and my own research has taught me that a successful school experience is bigger than this – as teachers we are not just teaching content to the mind, we are teaching young people to discover themselves. &nbsp;We feed their minds by providing experiences for them to learn how to learn, to problem – solve to synthesize and analyze. &nbsp;We feed their bodies and minds by providing healthy, happy environments where they feel accepted, welcomed and belong. &nbsp;We need to continually remind them that they have a contribution to make and we need to partner with them so they can discover what their passion is and how to develop a lifestyle that allows them to do whatever they want to do.</p>\n<p>For the past couple to years I was of the firm belief that implementing an inclusive education system could deliver on this agenda – an educational system where all students truly belonged and had successful experiences of making contributions. Recently I have been inspired and have learned that this dream of an inclusive education system isn’t big enough. &nbsp;Our schools not only need to focus on feeding the mind by teaching important learning skills and developing positive caring relationships we also need to ensure that we have healthy learning environments. &nbsp;Healthy learning environments ensure that our students are physically activity, eating healthy and have the social and emotional supports necessary for them to have a healthy emotional outlook.</p>\n<p>Over the past couple of weeks, I have integrated the philosophical underpinnings of an inclusive education system and the comprehensive school health curriculum. &nbsp;I have developed a dream about what this could mean for our students. &nbsp;In this dream I am envisioning what our schools could look like if we were to successfully integrate these two powerful agendas.</p>\n<p>I am a teacher but I also want to be a leader. &nbsp;Every day he presents a different law, provides some example and a practical application to daily living. &nbsp;He then prompts the reader to reflect on this law and apply the law to their own dir=\"ltr\"><strong>Law of Love</strong></p>\n<p dir=\"ltr\">This is Dr. Demartini’s first law. &nbsp;He teaches that everyone longs to be cherished for who they are, and that if you share the truth of your own heart the people you touch will become the wonderous individuals they’re capable of being. That is, love individuals for who they are, no matter what. &nbsp;He says that if you do they’ll become more, accomplish more, and have more in their lives – and so will you. &nbsp;The power of unconditional love is unsurpassed in its power to transform. &nbsp;If you were as lucky as me to be in the room with these teachers you would have heard them telling their stories about their students from a place of unconditional love. &nbsp;To these teachers every student was important, every student was valued and every student had something to offer. &nbsp;They expressed a desire and responsibility to create an environment where every one of their students could thrive.</p>\n<p dir=\"ltr\"><strong>Law of Reflection</strong></p>\n<p dir=\"ltr\">This is law number 42. A couple of stories that depicted the application of this law came from the stories that a kindergarten teacher and a middle years teacher told in their group. Both teachers talked about how a change in thinking allowed them to have the courage to allow their students to take responsibility for their learning. &nbsp;In the first story the teacher reflected on how she used to direct students through a particular task and when she turned it over to them to figure and stood back to observe, rather than direct amazing things happened. &nbsp;Real learning took place. &nbsp;In the second story, the teacher shared that where she once believed that there was one way to solve a problem – her students taught her that when they were encouraged to make their learning visible and they worked cooperatively together, many different pathways lead to the solution.</p>\n<p dir=\"ltr\">So the relevance of this book to my observations of the teacher’s discussions
