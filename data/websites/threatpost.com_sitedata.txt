Since the user has already entered their password in the Authenticator, it’s not needed. Instead all the user needs to do is unlock their phone, tap “Approve,” and they’re granted access.</p>\n<p>Alex Simons, the director of the company’s Identity Division announced the news in <a href=\"https://blogs.technet.microsoft.com/enterprisemobility/2017/04/18/no-password-phone-sign-in-for-microsoft-accounts/\">a blog post</a> Tuesday morning.</p>\n<twitterwidget class=\"twitter-tweet twitter-tweet-rendered\" id=\"twitter-widget-0\" data-tweet-id=\"854397158866288640\" style=\"position: static; visibility: visible; display: block; transform: rotate(0deg); max-width: 100%; width: 500px; min-width: 220px; margin-top: 10px; margin-bottom: 10px;\"></twitterwidget>\n<p><script async=\"\" src=\"//platform.twitter.com/widgets.js\" charset=\"utf-8\"></script></p>\n<p>If a user misplaces, loses, or has changed their device, they can choose to log in with their password on the confirmation page, Simons says.</p>\n<p>For now the feature is only available on iOS and Android devices. From there the user taps through to login.</p>\n<p>Facebook added a feature <a href=\"https://threatpost.com/facebook-touts-safer-security-key-login/123382/\">in January</a> that lets users tie a physical security key instead of a device to their accounts.</p>\n<p>Unlike Microsoft’s feature, users still need their passwords to login to the social network.
