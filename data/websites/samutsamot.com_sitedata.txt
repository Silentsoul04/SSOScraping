I&nbsp;have revised that list, added more entries, and categorized quite a lot of them. The link to the revised list (a PDF file) is provided below.</p>\n<p>A Filipino noun (<strong>pangngalan</strong>) may be categorized according the natural gender (male or female/<strong>lalaki o babae</strong>), the uncertainty of gender (male <em>or</em> female), or the lack of gender (nouns for nonliving things or concepts) of the person, animal, object, or idea the noun is pertaining to.</p>\n<p>A Filipino noun may be classified as having one of the four genders: masculine gender (<strong>panlalaki</strong>), feminine gender (<strong>pambabae</strong>), common gender (<strong>pambalaki o di-tiyak</strong>), or neuter gender (<strong>walang kasarian</strong>).</p>\n<p>Nouns with masculine gender are used for male persons and animals such as the nouns&nbsp;<strong>father/ama</strong> and <strong>rooster/tandang</strong>. Nouns with feminine&nbsp;gender are used for female persons and animals such as the nouns&nbsp;<strong>mother/ina</strong> and <strong>hen/inahin</strong>. Nouns with common gender are used for either male or female persons and animals such as the nouns <strong>parent/magulang</strong> and <strong>chicken/manok</strong>.</p>\n<p>Nouns with masculine, feminine, and common genders are used for people and animals. The nouns with neuter gender are used for nonliving things, living things that are not classified as either male or female (such as trees and plants), and abstract nouns. The Filipino nouns <strong>puno</strong>, <strong>kahoy</strong>, <strong>bulaklak</strong>, and <strong>kalikasan</strong> are examples of nouns with neuter gender.</p>\n<p>Some nouns&nbsp;with&nbsp;masculine gender are paired with their corresponding&nbsp;nouns with feminine gender. A tabular list of such Filipino noun pairs is provided in the PDF file below entitled “Kasarian ng mga Pangngalan sa Filipino.” Several of these Filipino noun pairs originated from the&nbsp;Spanish language.</p>\n<p>Note that the noun pairs in this table are <strong>gender-specific. </strong>This means that&nbsp;Filipino nouns under the column heading <strong>“Pangngalang panlalaki”</strong> may only be used to refer to male persons (or animals) and those under the column heading <strong>“Pangngalang pambabae”</strong> may only be used to refer to female persons (or animals).</p>\n<p>Filipino nouns such as <strong>doktor</strong>, <strong>senador</strong>, <strong>alkalde</strong>, and <strong>arsobispo</strong> have common gender; they may be used to refer to either men or&nbsp;women who hold these positions. (There are women in other parts of the world who hold religious occupations such as ministers, bishops, and priests.) So I have classified such nouns under the category <strong>pangngalang pambalaki </strong>or<strong>&nbsp;</strong>nouns with common gender.</p>\n<p>In my previous list I incorrectly&nbsp;categorized&nbsp;the Filipino nouns <strong>arsobispo</strong>, <strong>obispo</strong>, <strong>pari</strong> and other religious occupations under nouns with masculine gender. Filipino nouns like <strong>empleyado</strong>, <strong>propesor</strong>, and <strong>eredero</strong> were similarly categorized incorrectly under <strong>pangngalang panlalaki.</strong>&nbsp;These nouns actually have the common gender.</p>\n<p>The Filipino nouns <strong>doktora</strong>, <strong>senadora</strong>, <strong>alkaldesa</strong>, <strong>empleyada</strong>, <strong>propesora</strong>, and <strong>eredera</strong>&nbsp;have feminine gender. These nouns are gender-specific; they refer to women (not men) who hold these positions.</p>\n<p>Nouns with the neuter gender are nouns used for living and nonliving things that may not be classified as either male or female. Nouns that refer to places and inanimate objects, as well as abstract nouns, have the neuter gender.</p>\n<p>The PDF file below has 5 pages. This topic also appears in some Grade 6 Filipino textbooks.</p>\n<p>Adjectives or adjective phrases are used to describe nouns (<strong>pangngalan</strong>) or pronouns (<strong>panghalip</strong>) in a sentence. An adverb (<strong>pang-abay</strong>) describes or modifies a verb, an adjective, or another adverb. If the word describes a verb, then it is not an adjective; it is an adverb. To make sure that a word is used as an adjective or adverb, it is useful to first identify the word in the sentence that it is describing.</p>\n<p>The first PDF file below discusses the three main types of Filipino adjectives which are <strong>pang-uring panlarawan</strong> (descriptive adjective), <strong>pang-uring pantangi</strong> (proper adjective), and <strong>pang-uring pamilang</strong> (numeral adjective). There are also six types of<strong> pang-uring pamilang</strong> described here. A gave a few examples for each type.</p>\n<p>I’d like to stress that I am not Filipino teacher and the discussion I made is based on my own research and interpretation.
