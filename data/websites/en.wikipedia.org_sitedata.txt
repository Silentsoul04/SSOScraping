History\">edit</a><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class=\"mw-headline\" id=\"1905.E2.80.931985\">1905–1985</span><span class=\"mw-editsection\"><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">[</span><a href=\"/w/index.php?title=Orange_County_Register&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2\" title=\"Edit section: 1905–1985\">edit</a><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <i>Register</i> was founded by a consortium as the <i>Santa Ana Daily Register</i> in 1905. It was sold to J.P. Baumgartner in 1906 and to J. Frank Burke in 1927. In 1935 it was bought by <a href=\"/wiki/Raymond_C._Hoiles\" title=\"Raymond C. Hoiles\">Raymond C. Hoiles</a>, who renamed it the <i>Santa Ana Register</i> and reorganized his holdings as Freedom Newspapers, Inc., in 1950, later Freedom Communications. The paper dropped \"Santa Ana\" from its title in 1952.</p>\n<p>In 1956, the newspaper was a prominent supporter of a vociferous campaign by anti-communists against the <a href=\"/wiki/Alaska_Mental_Health_Enabling_Act\" title=\"Alaska Mental Health Enabling Act\">Alaska Mental Health Enabling Act</a>, claiming that it was part of a Communist plot to establish concentration camps in <a href=\"/wiki/Alaska\" title=\"Alaska\">Alaska</a>.</p>\n<p>Circulation rose with the burgeoning population of <a href=\"/wiki/Orange_County,_California\" title=\"Orange County, California\">Orange County</a> and after the <i>Register</i> added a morning edition in 1959. In 1970 Hoiles' son, Clarence, became co-publisher with his brother<sup class=\"noprint Inline-Template\" style=\"margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;\">[<i><a href=\"/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify\" title=\"Wikipedia:Please clarify\"><span title=\"The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2013)\">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Harry until 1979, when R.&nbsp;David Threshie, Clarence's son-in-law, was named to the position.</p>\n<p>Faced with an aggressive push into the county by the <i><a href=\"/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times\" title=\"Los Angeles Times\">Los Angeles Times</a></i> under then-publisher Otis Chandler, Threshie brought in 30-year-old N. Christian Anderson III as editor. Political positions were restricted to the editorial page. In 1981, the paper began publishing in full color.<sup class=\"noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact\" style=\"white-space:nowrap;\">[<i><a href=\"/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed\" title=\"Wikipedia:Citation needed\"><span title=\"This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)\">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<h3><span class=\"mw-headline\" id=\"1985.E2.80.932009\">1985–2009</span><span class=\"mw-editsection\"><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">[</span><a href=\"/w/index.php?title=Orange_County_Register&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3\" title=\"Edit section: 1985–2009\">edit</a><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1985, the paper assumed the name <i>The Orange County Register</i>. In the same year it won its first <a href=\"/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize\" title=\"Pulitzer Prize\">Pulitzer Prize</a>, for its photographic coverage of the <a href=\"/wiki/1984_Summer_Olympics\" title=\"1984 Summer Olympics\">1984 Summer Olympics</a> in Los Angeles. It won additional Pulitzers in 1989 for beat reporting by Edward Humes on U.S. military problems with <a href=\"/wiki/Night_vision_device\" title=\"Night vision device\">night-vision goggles</a> and in 1996 for an investigation into <a href=\"/wiki/Ricardo_Asch\" title=\"Ricardo Asch\">Ricardo Asch</a>'s fertility clinics.<sup id=\"cite_ref-4\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-4\">[4]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In 1990, the newspaper launched the 24-hour OCN news channel with news and feature stories about Orange County. It closed in 2001.<sup id=\"cite_ref-5\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-5\">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In 1992, Orange County Register Communications launched <i><a href=\"/wiki/Exc%C3%A9lsior\" title=\"Excélsior\">Excélsior</a>,</i> a Spanish-language weekly. In 2010 <i>Excélsior</i> had a circulation of 51,000.<sup id=\"cite_ref-6\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-6\">[6]</a></sup> It covers Orange County's growing Hispanic community, which now numbers over a million. Julio Saenz is the editor and general manager.</p>\n<p>In 1999, Threshie became Chairman of the Board of Freedom Communications, and N. Christian Anderson III assumed the position of publisher and <a href=\"/wiki/Chief_executive_officer\" title=\"Chief executive officer\">chief executive officer</a>. Ken Brusic was named vice president of content and executive editor in April 2002.<sup id=\"cite_ref-7\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-7\">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In 2003, a family schism led to a sale on Oct. 9th of a majority interest in Freedom Communications to investors led by the <a href=\"/wiki/Blackstone_Group\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Blackstone Group\">Blackstone Group</a> and <a href=\"/wiki/Providence_Equity_Partners\" title=\"Providence Equity Partners\">Providence Equity Partners</a>. Through a stock arrangement, the Hoiles family descendants retained control of the board.</p>\n<p>In 2006, Orange County Register Communications launched the <i><a href=\"/wiki/OC_Post\" title=\"OC Post\">OC Post</a></i>, a tabloid with shortened versions of <i>Register</i> stories as well as news articles from the <a href=\"/wiki/Associated_Press\" title=\"Associated Press\">Associated Press</a>. The paper also had its first significant staff reductions in December 2006, with 40 newsroom employees taking buyouts, along with a small number of layoffs.</p>\n<p>By April 2007, <i>The Orange County Register</i> had made cuts to help maintain shareholder profit, which had averaged more than 20 percent annually in the preceding five years. Since the launch of the <i><a href=\"/wiki/OC_Post\" title=\"OC Post\">OC Post</a></i> in 2006, OCRC<sup class=\"noprint Inline-Template\" style=\"margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;\">[<i><a href=\"/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify\" title=\"Wikipedia:Please clarify\"><span title=\"The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2013)\">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> has cut the <i>Register's</i> editorial staff by 10 percent, eliminated its 3% holiday bonuses for editorial staff. and postponed pay raises to editorial staff, which had averaged 3 percent annually, for six months.<sup class=\"noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact\" style=\"white-space:nowrap;\">[<i><a href=\"/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed\" title=\"Wikipedia:Citation needed\"><span title=\"This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2008)\">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In September 2007, Terry Horne replaced N. Christian Anderson III as publisher.</p>\n<p>In June 2008, <a href=\"/wiki/KTLA\" title=\"KTLA\">KTLA</a>, <i><a href=\"/wiki/The_Los_Angeles_Times\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"The Los Angeles Times\">The Los Angeles Times</a></i> and <a href=\"/wiki/Fox_News\" title=\"Fox News\">Fox News</a> reported that the <i>Register</i> had begun a one-month trial of outsourcing some layout and copy-editing work to India to save costs.<sup id=\"cite_ref-8\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-8\">[8]</a></sup> The trial was not deemed a success, and since then editing has been done by the <i>Register</i> in Orange County. In spring of 2009, <a href=\"/wiki/Freedom_Communications\" title=\"Freedom Communications\">Freedom Communications</a> instituted furloughs for all employees nationwide, followed by a permanent 5% pay cut starting in July 2009. News reports in August 2009 indicated that Freedom Communications planned to file for bankruptcy and turn control of its publications, including <i>The Orange County Register</i>, over to its lenders.<sup id=\"cite_ref-9\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-9\">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In September 2009, a column written by sports columnist Mark Whicker caused controversy.<sup id=\"cite_ref-10\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-10\">[10]</a></sup> In the column,<sup id=\"cite_ref-11\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-11\">[11]</a></sup> Whicker wrote about various sporting events that had occurred over the preceding 18 years, and how they had been missed by <a href=\"/wiki/Jaycee_Dugard\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Jaycee Dugard\">Jaycee Dugard</a>, a girl who had been kidnapped, raped, and forced to bear her kidnapper's children. Whicker ended his column with the line \"Jaycee, you have left the yard.\" The column generated widespread criticism and was parodied in blogs such as <a href=\"/wiki/Deadspin\" title=\"Deadspin\">Deadspin</a>,<sup id=\"cite_ref-12\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-12\">[12]</a></sup> who called it \"the single worst piece of journalism ever committed on this page,\" and the <a href=\"/wiki/Huffington_Post\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Huffington Post\">Huffington Post</a>.</p>\n<h3><span class=\"mw-headline\" id=\"2012.E2.80.93present\">2012–present</span><span class=\"mw-editsection\"><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">[</span><a href=\"/w/index.php?title=Orange_County_Register&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4\" title=\"Edit section: 2012–present\">edit</a><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On July 25, 2012, <i>The Orange County Register</i> and six other papers were purchased by 2100 Trust LLC.<sup id=\"cite_ref-13\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-13\">[13]</a></sup> The papers continued to operate under the Freedom Communications name.<sup id=\"cite_ref-FormerOC_14-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-FormerOC-14\">[14]</a></sup> In December the <i>Register</i> changed its logo and branding, dropping \"<i>The</i>\" in favor of <i>Orange County Register</i>.<sup id=\"cite_ref-no-the_15-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-no-the-15\">[15]</a></sup></p>\n<p>A lawsuit was filed in October 2013 by the former owners of Freedom Communications against <a href=\"/w/index.php?title=Aaron_Kushner&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1\" class=\"new\" title=\"Aaron Kushner (page does not exist)\">Aaron Kushner</a>, principal of 2100 Trust, demanding that Kushner's company pay more than $17 million remaining on the sale. The <i>Los Angeles Times</i> wrote that Kushner, \"a former greeting-card executive with no prior media experience,\" claimed that the prior owners had given him \"inaccurate valuations for a host of crucial financial indicators\" and that he faced \"$62.3 million in unexpected financial liabilities as a result.\"<sup id=\"cite_ref-FormerOC_14-1\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-FormerOC-14\">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<p>On August 19, 2013, the <i>Long Beach Register</i> was launched as an edition of the <i>Orange County Register</i> serving the <a href=\"/wiki/Long_Beach,_California\" title=\"Long Beach, California\">Long Beach, California</a>, community. It was focused solely on community news, including city government, public and private education, local sports coverage, business and entertainment as an intended competitor to the <i><a href=\"/wiki/Long_Beach_Press-Telegram\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Long Beach Press-Telegram\">Long Beach Press-Telegram</a></i>. In addition, on January 20, 2014, <i><a href=\"/wiki/The_Press-Enterprise\" title=\"The Press-Enterprise\">The Press-Enterprise</a></i> became an edition of the <i>Orange County Register</i> while maintaining coverage of the <a href=\"/wiki/Inland_Empire\" title=\"Inland Empire\">Inland Empire</a>.<sup id=\"cite_ref-RPE_16-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-RPE-16\">[16]</a></sup></p>\n<p>On April 16, 2014, the <i>Orange County Register</i> launched the <i>Los Angeles Register</i>, \"more a print play than a digital one\" serving <a href=\"/wiki/Los_Angeles_County\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Los Angeles County\">Los Angeles County</a>. It was the first time since the <i><a href=\"/wiki/Los_Angeles_Herald-Examiner\" title=\"Los Angeles Herald-Examiner\">Herald-Examiner</a></i> folded on November 1, 1989, that a main competitor to the <i><a href=\"/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times\" title=\"Los Angeles Times\">Los Angeles Times</a></i> was launched, this time intended to be \"as local as one edition can be for the entire county.\"<sup id=\"cite_ref-LAR1_17-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-LAR1-17\">[17]</a></sup> Five months later, Kushner announced in a company memo that the <i>Los Angeles Register</i> was ending publication effective immediately. Kushner wrote that \"pundits and local competitors\" will be quick to call the effort a failure while he believes that \"not taking bold steps toward growth\" would have been the true failure.<sup id=\"cite_ref-LAR2_18-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-LAR2-18\">[18]</a></sup> The <i>Long Beach Register</i> became a Sunday-only publication in June 2014,<sup id=\"cite_ref-19\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-19\">[19]</a></sup> and ceased publication in December 2014.<sup id=\"cite_ref-LBRstops_20-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-LBRstops-20\">[20]</a></sup> In October the <i>Los Angeles Times</i> sued the <i>Register</i> for failing to pay more than $2 million to the Times for delivery services for the now defunct Register newspapers in Los Angeles and Long Beach. In March 2014 the Los Angeles Superior Court granted the <i>Times</i> a $4.2 million <a href=\"/wiki/Writ_of_attachment\" title=\"Writ of attachment\">writ of attachment</a> to secure the ability of the <i>Times</i> to enforce a possible judgment in its favor.<sup id=\"cite_ref-21\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-21\">[21]</a></sup></p>\n<p>On March 10. 2015, Aaron Kushner and his partner, Eric Spitz, resigned from executive duties at the paper and Freedom Communications Inc. The company was rumored to be readying itself for a potential sale. Publisher Rich Mirman, a former Las Vegas casino executive who had invested in Freedom, was announced as the new president and chief executive.<sup id=\"cite_ref-22\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-22\">[22]</a></sup></p>\n<p>On February 12, 2016, Freedom Communications announced that the <i>Orange County Register</i> and the <i>Press-Enterprise</i> along with its websites, community weeklies and the two Spanish-Language weeklies <i>Excelsior</i> in Orange County and <i>La Prensa</i> in the Inland Empire, were being placed in a \"stalking horse\" auction after the company declared bankrupt at the end of 2015. Both Digital First Media and Tribune Publishing were the bidders. The auction started on March 21 and was completed on March 31, 2016.</p>\n<p>On March 21, 2016, <a href=\"/wiki/Digital_First_Media\" title=\"Digital First Media\">Digital First Media</a> acquired both the <i>Orange County Register</i> and the <i>Press-Enterprise</i> for $52.3 million in a U.S. Bankruptcy Court in <a href=\"/wiki/Santa_Ana,_California\" title=\"Santa Ana, California\">Santa Ana</a>. The U.S. Department of Justice blocked the sale of Freedom Communications to <a href=\"/wiki/Tribune_Publishing\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Tribune Publishing\">Tribune Publishing</a> because it would create a newspaper monopoly in both Orange and Riverside Counties. Los Angeles News Group was renamed Southern California News Group on March 31, 2016, once the sale of Freedom Communications to Digital First Media was completed. It has 11 paid regional dailies, and community weeklies serving the South Bay communities of Hermosa Beach, Redondo Beach and Palos Verdes Peninsula, the Long Beach neighborhoods north and east of downtown and over 20 community weeklies in Orange County, as well as the Spanish-language weeklies <i>Impacto USA</i> and <i>Unidos</i>.<sup id=\"cite_ref-23\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-23\">[23]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class=\"mw-headline\" id=\"Editorial_stances\">Editorial stances</span><span class=\"mw-editsection\"><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">[</span><a href=\"/w/index.php?title=Orange_County_Register&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5\" title=\"Edit section: Editorial stances\">edit</a><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <i>Register</i> was notable for its generally <a href=\"/wiki/Libertarianism\" title=\"Libertarianism\">libertarian</a>-leaning editorial page.<sup id=\"cite_ref-24\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-24\">[24]</a></sup> Although it sometimes supported Republican politicians and positions, it was the largest newspaper in the country to have opposed the <a href=\"/wiki/Iraq_war\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Iraq war\">Iraq war</a> from the beginning and opposed laws regulating issues such as prostitution and <a href=\"/wiki/Drug_addiction\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Drug addiction\">drug use</a>. It was one of a handful of newspapers that opposed the <a href=\"/wiki/Japanese_American_internment\" class=\"mw-redirect\" title=\"Japanese American internment\">internment of Japanese aliens and Japanese-Americans</a> during <a href=\"/wiki/World_War_II\" title=\"World War II\">World War II</a>.<sup id=\"cite_ref-LA_Times_25-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-LA_Times-25\">[25]</a></sup><sup id=\"cite_ref-NY_Times_26-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-NY_Times-26\">[26]</a></sup> It also opposed <a href=\"/wiki/California_Proposition_8_(2008)\" title=\"California Proposition 8 (2008)\">Proposition 8</a> in 2008, which proposed a ban on <a href=\"/wiki/Same-sex_marriage\" title=\"Same-sex marriage\">same-sex marriage</a>.<sup id=\"cite_ref-No_on_Prop_8_27-0\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-No_on_Prop_8-27\">[27]</a></sup> After the Digital First purchase of Freedom Communications, the <i>Register'</i>s editorial page was merged with that of the <i>Los Angeles Daily News</i> to form a single editorial board for the <a href=\"/wiki/Southern_California_News_Group\" title=\"Southern California News Group\">Southern California News Group</a> on regional and national issues.<sup id=\"cite_ref-28\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-28\">[28]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class=\"mw-headline\" id=\"Online_content\">Online content</span><span class=\"mw-editsection\"><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">[</span><a href=\"/w/index.php?title=Orange_County_Register&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6\" title=\"Edit section: Online content\">edit</a><span class=\"mw-editsection-bracket\">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On April 1, 2013, the <i>Orange County Register</i> began providing its online content through a metered <a href=\"/wiki/Paywall\" title=\"Paywall\">paywall</a>. Most online content required a subscription, with the exception of local weather, traffic, Associated Press or non-<i>Register</i> articles, and a few select local news articles.<sup id=\"cite_ref-29\" class=\"reference\"><a href=\"#cite_note-29\">[29]</a></sup> As of October 2015, the website does not have a paywall and online content is free.</p>\n<h2><span class=\"mw-headline\"
