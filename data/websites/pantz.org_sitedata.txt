This was so we could thwart things\nlike eavesdropping and content hijacking. In 2016 about about half of website visits\nwhere https. This is great and things seem to be only getting better. ISP's can not\nsee the content in https traffic. Not seeing your traffic content anymore makes \nthem sad. What makes them happy? They can still see all of your DNS requests.</p>\n\n<h3>Your ISP can see the websites you visit</h3>\n\n<p>Every ISP assigns you some of their DNS servers for you to use when you connect\nto them for your internet connection. Every time you type in a website name in your\nbrowser bar, this request goes to their DNS servers to look up an number called an\nIP address. After this happens an IP address is returned to your computer, and the \nconnection to the website is made. Your ISP now has a log of the website you \nrequested attached to the rest of the information they have about you. Then they\nbuild profiles about you, and sell that info to 3rd parties to target advertising\nto you in many different ways. Think you'll be slick and switch out their DNS \nservers with someone else's like Google's free DNS servers (8.8.8.8)? Think again.\nAny request through your ISP to any DNS server on the internet is unencrypted.\nYour ISP can slurp up all same requests and get the same info they did just\nlike when you were using their DNS servers. Just like when they could see all\nof your content with http before https. This also means that your DNS traffic\ncould possibly be intercepted and hijacked by other people or entities.\nTLS prevents this.</p>\n\n<h3>Securing the DNS</h3>\n\n<p>The thing that secures https is \n<a href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transport_Layer_Security\">\nTransport Layer Security (TLS)</a>. It is a set of cryptographic protocols that \nprovides communications security over a computer network. Now that we are \nbeginning to secure the websites I think it is high time we\nsecure the DNS. Others seem to agree. In 2016 <a href=\"https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc7858\">\nrfc7858</a> and <a href=\"https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc8094\">rfc8094</a> was \nsubmitted to Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF) which describes the use \nof DNS over TLS and DNS over DTLS. Hopefully these will eventually become a\nstandard and all DNS traffic will be more secure in transit. Can you have \nDNS over TLS today? Yes you can!</p>\n\n<h3>Trying DNS over TLS now</h3>\n\n<p>DNS over TLS is in its infancy currently, but there are ways to try it out now.\nYou could try using <a href=\"https://getdnsapi.net/blog/dns-privacy-daemon-stubby/\">Stubby</a>,\na program that acts as a local DNS Privacy stub resolver (using DNS-over-TLS). \nYou will have compile Stubby on Linux or OS X to use it. You could also setup your \nown DNS server at home and point it to some upstream forwarders that support \nDNS over TLS. This is what I have done to start testing this. I use Unbound as my\nlocal DNS server on my lan for all of my client machines. I used the \n<a href=\"https://calomel.org/unbound_dns.html\">configuration settings</a> provided \nby our friends over at <a href=\"https://calomel.org\">Calomel.org</a> to setup \nmy Ubound server to use DNS over TLS. Here is a \n<a href=\"https://portal.sinodun.com/wiki/display/TDNS/DNS-over-TLS+implementations\">\nlist</a> of other open source DNS software that can use DNS over TLS. With my\nUnbound setup, all of my DNS traffic is secured from interception and modification. \nSo how is my testing going?</p>\n\n<h3>The early days</h3>\n\n<p>Since this is not a IETF standard yet, there are not a lot of providers of\nDNS over TLS resolvers. I have had to rearrange my list of DNS over TLS\nproviders a few times when some of the servers were just not resolving \nhostnames. The latency is also higher than using your local ISP's DNS servers\nor using someones like Googles DNS servers. This is not very noticeable since\nmy local DNS server caches the lookups. I have a feeling the generous providers\nof these DNS over TLS services are being overwhelmed and can not handle the load\nof the requests. This is where bigger companies come into play.</p>\n\n<p>Places like Google or OpenDNS do not support DNS\nover TLS yet, but I'm hoping that they will get on board with this. Google \nespecially since they have been big proponents of making all websites https.\nThey also have the infrastructure to pull this off. Even if someone like\nGoogle turned this on, that means they get your DNS traffic instead of\nyour ISP. Will this ever end?</p>\n\n<h3>Uggh, people can still see my traffic</h3>\n\n<p>Let's face it, if your connected to the internet, at some point someone \ngets to see where your going. You just have to choose who you want/trust to\ngive this information to. If you point your DNS servers to Google they get \nto see your DNS requests. If I point my DNS at these test DNS over TLS servers\nthen they get to see my DNS traffic. It seems like the lesser of 2 evils to\nsend your DNS to 2nd party DNS servers then to your ISP. If you use your \nISP's DNS servers they know the exact name attached to the IP address they \nassigned you and the customer that is making the query. I have been holding\noff telling you the bad news. https SNI will still give up the domain names you\nvisit.</p>\n\n<p>Through all of this even if you point your DNS traffic to a DNS over TLS\nserver your ISP can still see many of the sites you go to. This is thanks to\nsomething in https called \n<a href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Server_Name_Indication\">Server Name \nIndication (SNI)</a>. When you make a connection to an https enabled website\nthere is a process called a handshake. This is the exchange of information \nbefore the encryption starts. During this unencrypted handshake (ClientHello) \none of the things that is sent by you is a remote host name. This allows the \nserver on the other end to choose appropriate certificate based on the \nrequested host name. This happens when multiple virtual hosts reside on \nthe same server, and this a very common setup. Unfortunately, your ISP \ncan see this, slurp it up, and log this to your account/profile. So now what?\n</p>\n\n<p>Would a VPN help? Yes, but remember now your DNS queries go to your VPN provider.\nWhat is nice is your ISP will not see any of your traffic anymore. That pesky\nSNI issue mentioned above goes away when using a VPN. But now your trusted\nendpoint is your VPN provider. They now can log all the sites you go to. So \nchoose wisely when picking a VPN provider. Read their policy on saving logs,\nchoose one that will allow you to pay with Bitcoin so you will be anonymous\nas possible. With a VPN provider you also have to be careful about DNS leaking.\nIf your VPN client is not configured right, or you forget to turn it on or, any\nother myriad of ways a VPN can fail, your traffic will go right back to your ISP.\n</p>\n\n<h3>Even VPN's don't make you anonymous</h3>\n\n<p>So you have encrypted your DNS and web traffic with TLS and your using a VPN. \nGood for you, now your privacy is a bit better, but not anonymity. Your still \nbeing tracked. This time it is AD networks and services you use. I'm not going\nto go into this as \n<a href=\"https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2009/09/new-cookie-technologies-harder-see-and-remove-wide\">many</a> \n<a href=\"https://www.consumer.ftc.gov/articles/0042-online-tracking\">other people</a>\nhave written on this topic. Just know that your being tracked one way or another.</p>\n\n<p>I know this all seems hopeless, but securing the web's infrasturcture bit by bit \nhelps improve privacy just a little more. DNS like http is unencrypted. There was\na big push to get websites to encrypt their data, now there needs to be the same\nattention given to DNS.</p>\n\n\n<script async=\"\" src=\"//pagead2.googlesyndication.com/pagead/js/adsbygoogle.js\">\n</script>\n<!-- AutoResponsive_Links_Only -->\n<ins class=\"adsbygoogle\" style=\"display: block; height: 60px;\" data-ad-client=\"ca-pub-8796103469637220\" data-ad-slot=\"9126877610\" data-ad-format=\"auto\" data-adsbygoogle-status=\"done\"><ins id=\"aswift_1_expand\" style=\"display:inline-table;border:none;height:60px;margin:0;padding:0;position:relative;visibility:visible;width:595px;background-color:transparent\"><ins id=\"aswift_1_anchor\" style=\"display:block;border:none;height:60px;margin:0;padding:0;position:relative;visibility:visible;width:595px;background-color:transparent\"><iframe width=\"595\" height=\"60\" frameborder=\"0\" marginwidth=\"0\" marginheight=\"0\" vspace=\"0\" hspace=\"0\" allowtransparency=\"true\" scrolling=\"no\" allowfullscreen=\"true\" onload=\"var i=this.id,s=window.google_iframe_oncopy,H=s&amp;&amp;s.handlers,h=H&amp;&amp;H[i],w=this.contentWindow,d;try{d=w.document}catch(e){}if(h&amp;&amp;d&amp;&amp;(!d.body||!d.body.firstChild)){if(h.call){setTimeout(h,0)}else if(h.match){try{h=s.upd(h,i)}catch(e){}w.location.replace(h)}}\" id=\"aswift_1\" name=\"aswift_1\" style=\"left:0;position:absolute;top:0;\"></iframe></ins></ins></ins>\n<script>\n(adsbygoogle = window.adsbygoogle || []).push({});\n</script>\n\n<p>\n<a href=\"http://del.icio.us/post?url=http://www.pantz.org/software/unbound/dns_over_tls_secure_the_dns.html&amp;title=DNS over TLS. People sending mail from Gmail to pantz.org \nwere getting bounce messages, which looks bad. All other email from any other\ndomain was coming in without issue. I have been forwarding email from Gmail\naccounts for many years now without issue.</p>\n\n<h3>Time to recheck settings</h3>\n\n<ol>\n<li>DNS A,AAAA and PTR records for IPV4,6 setup and work correctly</li>\n<li>SPF record setup correctly, but this is a forward so it always shows fail.\nThe bounce message passes SPF, so that's nice.</li>\n<li>SMTP w/TLS working and available</li>\n<li>pantz.org does not modify, remove or shuffle message headers or modify \nthe body of the message in any way.</li>\n<li>No increase in spam getting through. pantz.org filters most mail before \nit is forwarded.</li>\n<li>Not using Sender Rewriting Scheme (SRS).</li>\n<li>No DKIM setup</li>\n</ol>\n\n<h3>The fix</h3>\n<p> After seeing that everything checked out, I hit up Google to see if anyone\nelse was having this issue. From the results it seems that many people have\nhad this same issue. Some people just started using SRS to fix their issue.\nOthers had to fix their PTR records in DNS. The last group of people had to\nstop using IPV6 for mail delivery. Since all of the other pantz.org mail \nserver settings were correct, the only thing I could try was implementing\nSRS or turn off IPV6. Turning off IPV6 delivery was the easiest test.\nAfter turning off IPV6 mail delivery, and just leaving IPV4, all mail\nfrom Gmail being forwarded through pantz.org was now being accepted again.\nHow dumb is that?</p>\n\n<h3>What is up with IPV6?</h3>\n\n<p>It seems Gmail has changed a setting (or I hit some new threshold)\non their side dealing with only IPV6. Since Google will not tell you \nwhy certain mail is considered \"unsolicited mail\" we can not figure out \nwhat was done to try to fix the issue. If I had to speculate on what \nis happening, my guess is they turned up the sensitivity on email coming \nfrom IPV6 as it is obvious that IPV4 filter is not as sensitive. It is \nnot just my server as it is happening to many other people as well.</p>\n\n<p>I had also noticed that mail coming in from a friend whose server \ndelivers mail to my server via IPV6, and then was forwarded to Gmail\nvia IPV6 was being marked as spam every time. According to Google\nif the user is in your contacts list (and his email address is) the\nemail is not supposed to be marked as spam. That is straight broken.\nNow that I switched back to just IPV4 delivery, all of his mail is not\nbeing marked as spam anymore. I believe Google has an issue with IPV6\nmail delivery and spam classification.</p>\n\n<h3>What now?</h3>\n\n<p>I hate that I had to turn off IPV6 for mail forwarding to Gmail. Make sure it is set to run on start\n as well.</li> \n<li>Has the Minions key been accepted by the Master? If you don't even see a\nkey request from the Minion then the Minion is not even talking to the Master\n.</li>\n<li>Does the Minion have an unobstructed network path back to TCP port 4505 \non the Master? The Minions initialize a TCP connection back to \nthe Master so they don't need any ports open. Watch out for those Firewalls.\n</li>\n<li>Check your Minions log file in /var/log/salt/minion for key issues or \nany other issues.</li>\n</ol>\n\n<h3>Basic Communication</h3>\n<p>Now lets say you have all of basic network and and key issues worked out\nand would like to send some jobs to your Minions. You can do this via the\nSalt CLI. Something like salt \\* cmd.run 'echo HI'. \nThis is considered a job by Salt. The Minions get this request and run the\ncommand and return the job information to the Master. The CLI talks to\nthe Master who is listening for the return messages as they are coming \nin on the ZMQ bus. The CLI then reports back that status and output of the job.\n</p>  \n\n<p>That is a basic view of this process. But, sometimes Minions don't return job\ninformation. Then you ask yourself what the heck happened. You know the Minion\nis running fine. Eventually you find out you don't really understand \nMinion Master job communication at all.</p>\n\n<h3>Detailed Breakdown of Master Minion CLI Communication</h3>\n\n<p>By default when the job information gets returned to the Master and is \nstored on disk in the job cache. We will assume this is the case below.</p>\n\n<p>The Salt CLI is just an small bit of code that interfaces with the API \nSaltStack has written that allows anyone to send commands to the Minions \nprogrammatically. The CLI is not connected directly to the Minions when the\njob request is made. When the CLI makes a job request, is handed to the \nMaster to fulfill.</p>\n\n<p>There are 2 key timeout periods you need be aware of before we go into\na explanation of how a job request is handled. They are \"timeout\"\nand \"gather_job_timeout\".</p>\n\n<ul>\n<li>In the Master config the initial timeout \"timeout:\" setting tells the Master\nhow often to poll the Minions about their job status. After the initial timeout period\nexpires, the Master fires off a \"find_job\" query. If you did this on the command line \nit would look like salt \\* saltutil.find_job &lt;jid&gt; to all the Minions\nthat were targeted. This asks all of the Minions if they are still running the \njob they were assigned. This setting can also be set from the CLI with the\n\"-t\" flag. Do not make the mistake that this timeout is how long the CLI \ncommand will wait until it times out and kills itself. The default value for\n\"timeout\" is 5 seconds.</li>\n\n<li>In the Master config the \"gather_job_timeout\" setting defines how long\nthe Master waits for a Minion to respond to the \"find_job\" query issued by\nthe initial timeout setting mentioned above. If a Minion does not respond in the\ndefined gather_job_timeout time then it is marked by the Master as\n\"non responsive\" for that polling period. This setting can not be set from\nthe CLI. It can only be set in the Master config file. The default is 10\nseconds.</li>\n</ul>\n\n<p>When the CLI command is issued, the Master gathers a list of Minions with\nvalid keys so it knows which Minions are on the system. It validates and\nfilters the targeting information from the given target list and sets that as\nits list (targets) of Minions for the job. Now the Master has a list of who \nshould return information when queried. The Master takes the requested \ncommand, target list, job id, and a few pieces of info, and broadcasts a \nmessage on the ZeroMQ bus to all of Minions. When all Minions get the message,\nthey look at the target list and decide if they should execute the job or\nnot. If the Minion sees he is in the target list he executes the job. If\na Minion sees he is not part of the target list, he just ignores the message.\nThe Minion that decided to run the command creates an local job id for the job\nand then performs the work.</p>\n\n<p> While the Minions are working their jobs the CLI is waiting for the first\ninitial timeout period (-t or timeout:) to start. When that hits, the CLI sends\nsends the first \"find_job\" query. This kicks off the gather_job_timeout timer.\nThe Minions receive the the first find_job request with the original job_id.\nIf they are still running the job, the Minion responds to \"find job\" request \nwith a status of \"still working\" or \"Job Finished\". If a Minion does not respond\nto the request within the gather_job_timeout time period (10 secs), the CLI\nmarks the Minion as \"non responsive\" for the polling interval. All Minions \nwill keep being queried on the gather_job_timeout interval. If the Minions do\nnot reply within this timeout, or all report that they are no longer running\nthe job in question, the CLI command will return. If one of more minions \nreplies that they are still running the job, the initial timeout is triggered\nagain and the cycle repeats.</p>\n\n<p>The CLI will show the output from the Minions as they finish their jobs.\nFor the Minions that did not respond, but are connected to the Master, you\nwill see the message \"Minion did not return\". If a Minion does not even look\nlike it has a TCP connection with the Master, you will see \"Minion did not \nreturn. [Not connected]\".</p>\n\n<p>By this time the  Master should have marked the job as finished. The jobs\ninfo should now be available in the job cache. The above explanation is a high\nlevel explanation of how Master and Minions communicate. There are more details\nto this process than the above info, but this should give you a basic idea of how\nit works.</p>\n\n<h3>Takeaways From This Info</h3>\n<ol>\n<li>There is no defined period on how long a job will take. The job will finish\nwhen the last responsive Minion has said it is done.</li>\n<li>If a Minion is not up or connected when a job request it sent out, then\nthe Minion just misses that job. It is _not_ queued by the Master, and\nsent at a later time.</li>\n<li>Currently there is no hard timeout to force the Master to stop listening\n after a certain amount of time.</li>\n<li>If you set your timeout (-t) to be something silly like 3600, then if even\n one Minion is not responding the CLI will wait the full 3600 seconds to \nreturn. Beware!\n</li></ol>\n\n<h3>Missing Returns</h3>\n\n<p>Sometimes you know there are Minions up and working, but you get \n\"Minion did not return\" or you did not see any info from the Minion\nat all before the CLI timed out. It is frustrating, as you can send the same\nMinion that just failed a job and it finishes it with no problem.\nThere can be many reasons for this. Try/check the following things.</p>\n\n<ul>\n<li>Did the Minion actually get the job request message? Start the Minion\n with log level \"info\" or higher. Then check the Minion log /var/log/salt/minion\nfor job acceptance and completion.</li>\n<li>After the CLI has exited, check job log. Use the jobs.list_jobs runner\nto find the job id, then list the output of the job with the jobs.lookup_jid\nrunner. The CLI can miss returns, especially when the Master is overloaded.\nSee the next bit about a overloaded Master.</li> \n<li>The Master server getting overloaded is often the answer to missed returns.\nThe Master is bound mostly by CPU and disk IO. Make sure these are not being\nstarved. Bump up the threads in the Master config with the setting \n\"worker_threads\". Don't bump the threads past the amount of available CPU\ncores.\n</li>\n<li>Bump your timeout (-t) values higher to give the Minions a \nlonger start up period. </li>\n<li> Bump your gather_job_timeout value in the Master to give the Minions more \ntime in between find_job polling periods. This value becomes more important\nwhen your amount of Minions gets very high, as your polling period might be\nto short and your CLI/Master can't process all of the returns during the polling period.\nThen you end up overlapping polling periods. If your CLI or Master can't process the\nmessages coming in fast enough you will start getting missing returns.</li>\n</ul>\n\n<script async=\"\"
