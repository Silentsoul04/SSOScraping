680px) 100vw, 680px\"></a></p><p>In every industry, during every economy, there are businesses that thrive. There’s no mystery as to why this happens. We can even predict them in advance.</p><p>In fact, we can choose to become one of them. And that’s what today’s message is all about.</p><p>I’m very specifically referring to the business owners who:</p><ul><li>Have the courage to do things <a title=\"A remarkable lesson from Steve Jobs and Malcolm Gladwell\" href=\"http://jimsmarketingblog.com/2014/08/25/a-remarkable-lesson-from-steve-jobs-and-malcolm-gladwell/\" target=\"_blank\">their way</a>. As Warren Buffett says, the best investors sell when everyone else is buying, and buy when everyone else is selling.</li><li>Have the courage to turn away <a href=\"http://jimsmarketingblog.com/2014/03/18/avoid-bad-clients-late-payers-cash-flow-problems/\" target=\"_blank\">the wrong kind of clients</a>. They know that no business can thrive without a strong client base or customer base.</li><li>Have the courage to develop new products or services.</li><li>Have the courage to refuse to do average work. They know that people judge them based on the quality of their work. So they set the bar high and increase their value.</li><li>Have the courage to <a title=\"How to increase sales and build an excellent reputation\" href=\"http://jimsmarketingblog.com/2014/02/04/how-to-increase-sales-and-build-an-excellent-reputation/\" target=\"_blank\">set deadlines</a> and deliver as promised. They’ve figured out that this is one of the best ways to earn the trust of their clients.</li><li>Have the courage to charge 100% or 200% more for their time, than the industry average.</li><li>Have the courage to focus on what they want. They’ve learned that we tend to move in the direction of what we focus on. So they refuse to focus on what they fear.</li><li>Have <a href=\"http://jimsmarketingblog.com/2015/07/17/exposed-the-great-leadership-lie/\" target=\"_blank\">the courage to lead</a>. When everyone else is waiting for someone to take action, these business owners lead by example.</li></ul><p>Time and again, I find that the most common factor behind successful business owners and entrepreneurs, is courage.</p><h3>The role of courage in business success</h3><p>Fact: A courageous business owner will always outperform a fearful one. This holds true, no matter how smart the fearful business owner is.</p><p>For instance, many of the most fearful and least successful business owners I have met, were very highly educated. However, as former US president Calvin Coolidge famously said, “<a href=\"http://www.creativethinkinghub.com/educated-derelicts/\" target=\"_blank\">The world is full of educated derelicts</a>“. They were great at detail. Wonderful with research, yet lousy when it came to leaving their comfort zones. They lacked the courageous mindset of an entrepreneur.</p><h3>Smart and courageous</h3><p>Conversely, Apple was co-founded by a courageous university drop-out. And Steve Jobs was not alone. Dell founder Michael Dell never graduated. Nor did Henry Ford, Wendy’s CEO or Oracle co-founder Larry Ellison. (<em>Ellison is worth an estimated $54 Billion, around 10 times more than Sir Richard Branson</em>). Smart people for sure. I’ve found that nothing can stop a smart, courageous business owner from succeeding. She wanted to know if it was wise for her to raise awareness of a social issue she’s passionate about, via her business. She’s concerned that by taking a stance, she might lose potential customers.</p><p>As this is something that can apply to almost every business owner, I thought I’d share my answer with you.</p><p>For clarity, when I refer to a stance here, I’m talking about standing up for something that’s important to you, which is outside your business. Something you believe in. Something others will either agree with or disagree with. For example, a social issue, environmental cause or the work of a certain charity, etc.</p><p>Here are my thoughts, purely from a marketing perspective. As you’ll see there are upsides and downsides.</p><h3>Is it always a bad idea to alienate a subset of the marketplace?</h3><p>No. No it isn’t. Allow me to explain.</p><blockquote><p>As <a href=\"https://www.fastcompany.com/3061109/companies-dont-be-afraid-to-take-a-stand-it-can-be-good-for-your-brand\" target=\"_blank\">this article</a> from Fast Company confirms, “Customers <em><strong>care</strong></em> not only about what companies <em><strong>sell</strong></em> but also about what they <em><strong>stand for</strong></em>.”</p></blockquote><p>By taking a stance on something that really matters to you, you send some powerful signals to your marketplace.</p><ul><li>You show them that there are ethics behind your business. This is true whether they agree or disagree with your stance.</li><li>You show them that you’re willing to stand-up and lead. Again, this is true regardless of their opinion of your stance.</li><li>You show a segment of your marketplace that something they care passionately about, also matters to you. This forms a strong emotional connection between you and them. It transforms how they feel about you. This kind of connection is enormously valuable. That’s because people make decisions based on how they feel.</li></ul><p>In fact, feelings will usually have a more powerful influence on a buying decision, than facts.</p><p>If you’ve ever used proven facts, to try and convince someone you are right and they are wrong, you’ll know how powerful feelings are, compared to facts.</p><h3>A missed opportunity?</h3><p>Typically, small business owners avoid taking a stance. Yes, they have strong beliefs. We all do. However, they choose to keep them separate from their business.</p><p>Why?</p><p>Because they don’t want to risk alienating any prospective clients, who may hold opposing beliefs. So rather than allow a segment of their marketplace to form a powerful emotional connection with them, they remain silent about what truly matters to them.</p><blockquote><p>They strive to be vaguely <em><strong>relevant to</strong></em> <em><strong>everyone.</strong></em> And as an unavoidable result, they become directly <strong><em>relevant to no one</em></strong>.</p></blockquote><p>And it’s hard, really hard, to grow a business that’s only vaguely relevant.</p><h3>Marketing to everyone?</h3><p>I once asked a small business owner, who her ideal client was? She replied:</p><p>“Anyone with skin”.</p><p>And she was wrong.</p><p>For someone to become your client, they have to want (not necessarily need) what you provide. That’s an emotional decision. Sure, they may need a new website, but if they don’t want a new website or <em>they don’t want to hire you to design their new website</em>, they won’t hire you.</p><p>By taking a stance, by developing that stronger emotional connection, you give a segment of your marketplace a reason to want to hire you.</p><p>Does this mean that you’re guaranteed they will hire you? No. Of course not.</p><p>However, if (for example) they’re passionate about helping children in developing nations to have access to clean drinking water, and they see you are too, they’ll know you’re a kindred spirit. They’ll see that what matters to them, matters to you — that you share a worldview. And people prefer to do business with people, who share the same worldview. This is really important. Seth Godin’s <a href=\"http://sethgodin.typepad.com/seths_blog/2016/09/a-value-creation-checklist.html\" target=\"_blank\">value creation checklist</a> placed worldview right at the top.</p><h3>Big brands already get it</h3><p>Almost every major brand, visibly supports causes close to them. For example, Walmart takes an environmental stance and donated almost a third of a billion dollars last year alone. Google (Alphabet) takes a stance by supporting the Equal Justice Initiative. And Coca Cola takes a stance by supporting Africa’s Global Environment and Technology Foundation.</p><p>I was unable to find any major brand, that doesn’t visibly take a stand in support of a cause they are passionate about.</p><h3>Your stance can be part of your story</h3><p>Taking a stance is about making the things that matter most to you, a relevant and visible part of your work. It’s about building a story, which is consistent with your business and your guiding values / passions / ethics.</p><p>It isn’t easy. And it isn’t right for every business.</p><p>This is especially the case, if your stance is polarizing. That’s to say, a stance that most people will either strongly agree with or strongly disagree with. It’s not uncommon for businesses that take a polarizing stance, to receive abuse / trolling, from those passionately opposed to their stance.</p><p>Equally, if your business relies on customers from your local community and your stance is massively unpopular locally, the financial cost can be huge.</p><p>In short, there’s no universal right answer. Whether it’s right for you and your business depends on a number of factors. As with <a href=\"http://jimsmarketingblog.com/2016/03/16/business-decisions/\">every business decision</a>, it pays to do the research, get the feedback you need and then decide.</p></div><footer This commitment to being reliable is both rare and highly valued.</li><li>They are self-starters. They don’t seek external motivation. They just do what needs doing.</li><li>They are eager to take responsibility. They step up, when others step back.</li><li>They are intentional decision makers. Once they have the information required, they make a decision. Leaders know that there’s nothing to be gained by inaction.</li><li>They keep their promises. They know that making promises, then delivering on those promises, is a powerful way to develop loyalty and trust.</li><li>They spend their prime-time with family, friends or learning…not watching TV. Leaders are educated consumers of information. In other words, they are extremely careful about how they feed their mind.</li><li>They never gossip. Period.</li></ol><p>Bonus: Here are <a href=\"http://jimsmarketingblog.com/2014/03/22/12-proven-ways/\" target=\"_blank\">21 Powerful habits behind highly successful business class=\"entry-header\"><h2 class=\"entry-title\" itemprop=\"headline\"><a href=\"https://jimsmarketingblog.com/2017/03/31/marketing-tips-content-marketing-results/\" rel=\"bookmark\">The most incredible article about headlines you will ever read!</a></h2><p class=\"entry-meta\"><time class=\"entry-time\" itemprop=\"datePublished\" datetime=\"2017-03-31T03:33:53+00:00\">March 31, 2017</time> by <span class=\"entry-author\" itemprop=\"author\" itemscope=\"\" itemtype=\"http://schema.org/Person\"><a href=\"https://jimsmarketingblog.com/author/jimconnolly/\" class=\"entry-author-link\" itemprop=\"url\" rel=\"author\"><span class=\"entry-author-name\" itemprop=\"name\">Jim Connolly</span></a></span></p></header><div class=\"entry-content\" itemprop=\"text\"><p><a href=\"http://wp.me/pinHM-b64\"><img class=\"aligncenter size-full wp-image-47088\" src=\"http://jimsmarketingblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/06/content.jpg\" alt=\"marketing tips, marketing blogs, content\" width=\"640\" height=\"426\" srcset=\"https://jimsmarketingblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/06/content.jpg 640w, https://jimsmarketingblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/06/content-300x200.jpg 300w\" sizes=\"(max-width: 640px) 100vw, 640px\"></a></p><p>Here are some ideas, which you can use to dramatically improve the results of all your written marketing.</p><p>It’s all about the marketing&nbsp;power of&nbsp;<strong>headlines</strong>.</p><h3>Your headline&nbsp;has&nbsp;to capture the reader’s attention</h3><p>Headlines are important. Really important.</p><p>You see, it doesn’t matter how great your message is, if too few people ever read it. That’s where your headline (or title or subject line) comes in. The headline’s&nbsp;primary job is to attract attention, gain interest and then motivate the reader to carry on reading.</p><p>Think about it:</p><ul><li>The headline is&nbsp;what inspires prospective clients&nbsp;to open your marketing email.</li><li>It’s what motivates them to read your blog post or article, when someone shares it on a social network.</li><li>It’s what compels them to listen to your podcast or watch your video.</li><li>It’s also what grabs their attention and interest when they see one of your advertisements.</li></ul><p>Advertising legend&nbsp;<a href=\"http://www.ogilvy.com/About/Our-History/David-Ogilvy-Bio.aspx\" target=\"_blank\">David Ogilvy</a>&nbsp;was in no doubt regarding the importance of headlines. He famously&nbsp;said:</p><blockquote><p>“When you have written your headline, you have spent 80 cents out of your dollar.”</p></blockquote><p>Treat your headlines with the importance they deserve. Give yourself plenty of time to craft the best headline possible. The following tips and examples will help you get the balance right.</p><h3>Your headline should&nbsp;accurately reflect your content</h3><p>Because headlines play such a huge role in getting your content noticed, it’s tempting to over exaggerate them. (Yes, the headline of this post is a tongue in cheek example of what I mean).</p><p>Sometimes called <a href=\"https://en.oxforddictionaries.com/definition/clickbait\" target=\"_blank\">clickbait</a>, these attention-grabbing headlines are proven to generate traffic.&nbsp;<span style=\"line-height: 1.5;\">The reason I strongly recommend most people reading this not to adopt that headline strategy, is this:</span></p><blockquote><p>Your headlines make a promise, which your content needs to deliver on.</p></blockquote><p>Make your headlines as compelling as you can. Use words that will compel people to read what you have to say. But make darn sure that your content backs up the promise of the headline. You may be able to fool someone once or twice with clickbait. But unless your content delivers on the promise of the headline, people will quickly learn to ignore you.</p><h3>Your headline needs to be written for your target market</h3><p>Your marketing message is intended&nbsp;to connect with a very specific group of people: Your prospective clients or customers. The headlines you use should do the same. This means speaking their language and addressing their concerns and opportunities.</p><p>Here’s why this matters:</p><ul><li>By focusing your headlines around the interests of your target market, you help your marketing message to attract the attention of the right people.</li><li>Conversely, by using headlines that&nbsp;attract&nbsp;the attention of a wider&nbsp;group of people, you cease to be directly relevant to your target market.</li></ul><p>In other words, use headlines that are&nbsp;directly relevant to your prospective clients.</p><h3>Your headlines should match your&nbsp;medium</h3><p>If you’re writing a headline that’s intended for a print magazine, newspaper or flyer, you have certain freedoms, which you don’t have when writing for the internet.</p><p>For example, if you want your internet article’s headline&nbsp;to be fully displayed in search results, you need to use around 60 / 65 characters or fewer. Going beyond that will see your headline&nbsp;cut short.</p><p>If your headline&nbsp;is intended for email marketing, you need to take other things into consideration. For example, if you include exclamation marks!! in your subject line, along with a number and maybe a word that’s in ALL CAPS, it’s highly likely to end up in a lot of spam filters. Email software looks for certain common factors used by spammers and if it sees them in the email headlines you use, it could wrongly treat your marketing as spam.</p><p>Here’s the thing: When it comes to headlines, you need to adapt depending on the medium you’re using.</p><h3>The only 2 types of headline that matter</h3><p>There is a lot of debate among marketing professionals, regarding the correct way to use headlines. They take entrenched stances, each insisting that their approach is the only strategy that’s professional or effective.</p><p>In almost every case I have seen, they totally miss the point!</p><p>When it comes to the headlines you use in your marketing, there are just 2 broad categories worthy of your attention.</p><ol><li><strong>Headlines that work</strong> for you and your business goals.</li><li><strong>Headlines that are failing</strong> you and your business goals.</li></ol><p>You need to find the correct balance for what you want to achieve. And it will differ depending on your brand, your industry and your business model.</p><p>For example, <a href=\"http://www.upworthy.com/\" target=\"_blank\">Upworthy</a> (and especially <a href=\"http://www.buzzfeed.com/\" target=\"_blank\">BuzzFeed)</a> have&nbsp;grown into very successful media businesses, using a controversial, yet powerful headline&nbsp;formula. Many have labelled their approach as being linkbait or clickbait. Here’s a nice piece from <a href=\"http://www.theguardian.com/media/2014/mar/16/upworthy-website-generation-y-awful-headlines\" target=\"_blank\">The Guardian that looks at Upworthy’s headlines</a>.</p><p>Now, it’s not a strategy that would work for every business. However, it’s proven itself to be an extremely successful approach for the BuzzFeed business model. And that’s largely because the quality of the content is consistently compelling, so their readers return.</p><h3>In summary</h3><p>Experiment until you find what works best for you. Test and measure your headlines, titles and email subject lines. Just make sure that your content backs up the promise they make.</p><p>Get this balance right and you can dramatically and measurably improve your marketing results.</p></div><footer class=\"entry-footer\"><p class=\"entry-meta\"><span class=\"entry-categories\">Filed Under: <a href=\"https://jimsmarketingblog.com/category/blogs-marketing/\" rel=\"category tag\">Blogging</a>, <a href=\"https://jimsmarketingblog.com/category/development-business/\" rel=\"category tag\">Business Development</a>, <a href=\"https://jimsmarketingblog.com/category/copy-writing/\" rel=\"category tag\">Copywriting</a>, <a href=\"https://jimsmarketingblog.com/category/mailshots/\" rel=\"category tag\">Email marketing &amp;
